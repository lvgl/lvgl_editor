You design in Figma the way you always have, and [**LVGL Flow for Figma**](https://www.figma.com/community/plugin/1362005814860504095/lvgl-flow-for-figma) turns that design
into [LVGL XML](../syntax/introduction) files a developer can open, run, and refine. You don't write code to make
it happen.

## Start to finish

When you sit down to actually do this, the flow looks like this:

<Steps>

<Step>

**Open the plugin, and it scans your file.** A quick wizard reads through your tokens,
components, and screens so it knows what it's working with.

</Step>

<Step>

**Set your display size.** One number pair: the width and height of your device screen.
It's the single required setting, and exports wait until it's filled in.

</Step>

<Step>

**Annotate any widgets.** For a purely visual screen you can skip this. When a layer is
really a control or shows live data (e.g. a slider or a button), tell the plugin so in Annotate mode. It makes a smart
guess you confirm or adjust.

</Step>

<Step>

**Export.** Choose what to send (your tokens, your components, a few screens, or a whole
prototype flow) and the plugin hands it to the service.

</Step>

<Step>

**Files land on disk.** The service writes the LVGL UI files into your project, and you
open and refine them in LVGL Pro Editor. You can come back and export again any time; a
developer's hand edits stay untouched (see [Updating](./updating)).

</Step>

</Steps>

## Two halves that talk to each other

LVGL Flow has two parts, and it helps to picture them side by side.

- **The plugin** lives in Figma. It reads your file (your screens, components, colors,
  fonts, and spacing) and gets it ready to hand off.
- **The service** lives inside **[LVGL Pro Editor](../editor)**. It receives your design and writes the
  XML files into your project. You start it in the Editor with a simple click.

## What converts on its own, and what needs a nudge

- **A purely visual screen exports fully with no annotation.** Frames, text, images, shapes,
  and layout all come across on their own: your auto-layout becomes [LVGL flex](../syntax/view), your styling
  and colors and fonts convert as tokens, no extra steps.
- **Widgets are not auto-detected.** To get a functional LVGL widget (a slider, switch,
  checkbox, dropdown, bar, or text area) or any data binding, you must **annotate** that layer
  as the widget.

Annotation is **optional for visual and layout content, and required for widgets and
interactivity**.

## A little Figma fluency goes a long way

LVGL Flow reads the **structure** in your file, not just the pixels. So the more you lean
on Figma's building blocks, the cleaner the LVGL output.

- **Variables and styles**: your colors, fonts, and spacing saved as reusable values.
  LVGL Flow turns them into design **tokens** saved in `globals.xml` or in the XML file of the components and screens. [Guide to variables in Figma](https://help.figma.com/hc/en-us/articles/15339657135383-Guide-to-variables-in-Figma).
- **Components**: elements you build once and reuse. They come across as reusable [LVGL
  components](../syntax/components). [Guide to components in Figma](https://help.figma.com/hc/en-us/articles/360038662654-Guide-to-components-in-Figma).
- **Variants** are the states of a component: Default, Hover, Pressed, and so on. LVGL Flow
  can fold them into a single interactive widget whose look changes with its **state**. [Create and use variants](https://help.figma.com/hc/en-us/articles/360056440594-Create-and-use-variants).
- **Auto layout**: Figma's flex-style spacing and alignment. It maps directly onto LVGL's
  own flex layout, so responsive rows and columns translate instead of freezing into fixed
  positions. [Explore auto layout properties](https://help.figma.com/hc/en-us/articles/360040451373-Explore-auto-layout-properties).
